God With Us

Encountering Jesus in the Gospel of Matthew

An exploration based on the work of Edward Sri

Introduction: God with Us

Why Jesus Lived

To truly understand the monumental event of Jesus's death and resurrection, we must first ask a more fundamental question: *Why did Jesus live?* Edward Sri's "God With Us" invites us into the world of first-century Judaism to see Jesus as his contemporaries would have: the long-awaited Messiah-King. The central theme is that Jesus is "Emmanuel" (God with us ), whose entire life was a mission to restore humanity's broken communion with God. This theme is announced at the beginning of Matthew's Gospel and powerfully restated in its final words: "I am with you always, to the close of the age" (Matthew 28:20).

Key Concepts to Grasp:

  • Jesus as the Messiah-King: He is the fulfillment of the Davidic kingdom prophecies, a king establishing his kingdom on earth.
  • Meticulous Matthew: The Gospel is a masterfully structured catechesis, deeply rooted in the Old Testament, meticulously showing how Jesus fulfills every prophecy and becomes the New Moses, the New David, and the New Temple.

Part I: The King's Identity and Mission

Chapter 1: The Return of the King (Matthew 1)

A Genealogy of Hope

Jesus's genealogy is a powerful proclamation that God's promises to Israel are being fulfilled. By tracing Jesus's lineage to David and Abraham, Matthew establishes him as the rightful Son of David. The structure itself, in three sets of fourteen generations, is a Hebrew numerical wordplay on David's name (D-W-D = 14), emphasizing that Jesus is the ultimate Davidic King. The mention of the Babylonian Exile highlights the centuries of suffering and longing for a king to end their spiritual and political oppression. The genealogy climaxes with Jesus, the "Christ" (Messiah), whose name means "God saves," directly answering the problem of sin that caused the exile.

Modern Comparison: The Unveiling of a Lost Heir

Imagine a nation living under foreign rule for generations, its own royal line thought to be extinct. Suddenly, a historian unveils a meticulously documented family tree proving a living person is the rightful heir to the throne. This isn't just history; it's a political earthquake. That is the impact of Matthew 1 for its original audience.

Chapter 2: Fulfilling All Prophecy (Matthew 2)

Divine Providence in Every Detail

Matthew demonstrates that Jesus's life is no accident; it's the fulfillment of a divine script written in the Old Testament. He uses two key techniques: explicit fulfillment quotations (e.g., "This was to fulfill what the Lord had spoken by the prophet...") and implicit allusions (typology). Through typology, Jesus is presented as the "new and improved" version of Old Testament figures. He is the New Moses, escaping a murderous ruler (Herod/Pharaoh) and returning from Egypt to be a new liberator. The story of the Magi echoes the pagan prophet Balaam, who, despite a wicked king's intentions, prophesied a star that would announce the rise of a king from Jacob.

Chapter 3: The King's Anointing (Matthew 3)

Baptism as a Royal Coronation

Jesus's baptism is his royal anointing. John the Baptist, the New Elijah, calls Israel to a New Exodus at the Jordan River—the very place they first entered the Promised Land. When Jesus is baptized, the Holy Spirit descends upon him, just as the Spirit anointed the kings of old. The Father's voice from heaven, "This is my beloved Son," confirms his identity as the prophesied Messiah. This isn't an admission of sin; it's the inauguration of his kingdom.

The Spirit descends not just as a dove, but as an anointing oil, consecrating the King for his mission.

Chapter 4: Battle in the Desert (Matthew 4 )

The King Defeats the True Enemy

Jesus's first act as the anointed King is to battle Israel's true enemy: Satan. He becomes the New Israel, reliving and reversing Israel's 40 years of failure in the desert in 40 days of faithfulness. He faces three temptations that parallel Israel's key failures: trusting in bread over God, testing God's power, and idolatry. Where Israel failed, Jesus triumphs. This victory over sin prefigures his ultimate triumph on the Cross and shows that his kingdom will fight a spiritual, not a military, battle.

Part II: The Kingdom Proclaimed

Chapter 5: The Challenge of the Kingdom (Matthew 5-7)

A Counter-Cultural Manifesto

The Sermon on the Mount is Jesus's kingdom manifesto. In a time of national crisis, with groups like the Pharisees, Essenes, and Revolutionaries offering different paths, Jesus presents a radical New Vision. His teachings—"Blessed are the merciful," "Love your enemy"—were a shock to those expecting a military revolt. He challenges Israel to become a light to the nations, not a fortress against them. As the New Moses on a New Sinai, he presents two paths: the way of blessing (the Beatitudes) or the way of destruction, urging the people to choose life.

Modern Example: A CEO's Revolutionary Strategy

Imagine a failing company where consultants advise aggressive takeovers or mass layoffs. A new CEO arrives and instead calls for radical generosity, investing in the well-being of competitors and the community. This is the kind of shocking, upside-down strategy Jesus proposed for the "business" of God's kingdom.

Chapter 6: Speaking Louder than Words (Matthew 8-9)

Miracles as Prophetic Actions

After his sermon, Jesus performs ten miracles that are living parables. These are not just acts of kindness but prophetic signs fulfilling Isaiah's prophecies of Israel's restoration. By healing the unclean—lepers, a hemorrhaging woman—he demonstrates a holiness so powerful it cleanses rather than becomes contaminated, symbolizing his mission to restore the outcasts. By forgiving sins, he declares himself the New Temple, the true place of communion with God. By eating with sinners, he shows that his kingdom is a hospital for the spiritually sick.

Chapter 7: Conflict in Galilee (Matthew 10-12)

A New Leadership for a New Israel

As Jesus's popularity soars, so does the opposition. The Pharisees commit the unforgivable sin—blasphemy against the Spirit—by attributing God's work to Satan. Seeing that Israel's current shepherds are leading the flock astray, Jesus establishes a New Israel by choosing twelve apostles, a number symbolizing the twelve tribes. He grants them his own apostolic authority, telling them, "He who receives you receives me," thus laying the foundation for his Church.

Part III: The Mystery of the Kingdom and the King

Chapter 8: The Surprise of the Parables (Matthew 13)

Concealing to Reveal

Jesus shifts his teaching method to parables not to simplify his message, but to hide the secrets of the kingdom from the hard-hearted while revealing them to the receptive. The Parable of the Sower serves as a key: the different soils represent the varied responses to his ministry. The hardened path (Pharisees), the shallow rocky ground (superficial crowds), the thorny soil (the worldly), and the good soil (the disciples) all find their place in the story, forcing the audience to ask, "Which soil am I?"

Chapter 9: The Keys to the Kingdom (Matthew 14:1-16:19)

The Royal Prime Minister

At Caesarea Philippi, Jesus elevates Simon by giving him a new name, Peter ("Rock"), and the "keys of the kingdom." This act is rich with Old Testament meaning. The "Rock" alludes to the Temple's foundation stone, making Peter the unshakeable foundation of the Church. The "keys" were the symbol of the Davidic prime minister (Isaiah 22), who governed with the king's full authority. Jesus, the King, is appointing Peter as his prime minister. This office implied succession, establishing a lineage of authority to govern the Church throughout history.

The Keys represent divine authority; the Rock represents an unshakeable foundation.

Chapter 10: The Secret of the Messiah (Matthew 16:20-21:11 )

The Path to Glory is a Cross

Immediately after being recognized as the Messiah, Jesus reveals his "secret": he must be a suffering Messiah. He shatters popular expectations of a military conqueror by predicting his own death and resurrection. He commands his followers to "take up their cross," transforming an instrument of torture into the ultimate symbol of discipleship. The Transfiguration offers a glimpse of his glory to fortify the apostles for the trials ahead. His royal entry into Jerusalem on a donkey is a public, yet humble, claim to be the prophesied King who comes in peace, not war.

Part IV: The Climax of the Kingdom

Chapter 11: Jesus, the Temple, and the End Times (Matthew 21:12-25:46)

The End of an Age

Jesus's cleansing of the Temple is a prophetic act of judgment. He condemns it for failing its mission to be a "house of prayer for all nations" and becoming a "den of robbers" (a hub for nationalistic revolt). He then uses dramatic, apocalyptic language (stars falling, sun darkening) not to predict the end of the world, but the destruction of the Temple and the end of the Old Covenant world order. This prophecy was fulfilled with the Fall of Jerusalem in AD 70, within the "generation" he specified.

Chapter 12: The Trial of the King (Matthew 26:1-27:26)

The Accused Becomes the Judge

At his trial, Jesus turns the tables on his accusers. He identifies himself as the heavenly "Son of Man" from Daniel's prophecy, a figure who represents God's people, is persecuted by beasts (pagan empires), but is ultimately vindicated and given all authority. In this stunning move, Jesus casts the Jewish leaders as the beasts—the true enemies of God's people. The crowd's subsequent choice of Barabbas over Jesus symbolizes Israel's tragic choice of violent revolution over the way of the Cross.

Chapter 13: The Climax of the Cross (Matthew 27:27-66)

The Cross as the Throne of Victory

The Crucifixion is Jesus's enthronement as King. At the Last Supper, he becomes the New Passover Lamb, establishing a New Covenant in his blood. On the Cross, he enters into solidarity with sinners, taking upon himself the curses of Adam and Israel to redeem them. His sacrifice is the ultimate fulfillment of Abraham's offering of his beloved son, Isaac, on that same mountain range. This time, the sacrifice is completed, and the promised blessing flows out to all nations. This is not defeat; it is the ultimate victory.

Chapter 14: Great Commission (Matthew 28)

A Kingdom for All Nations

The Gospel culminates with the resurrected King, now vested with all authority, commissioning his apostles to expand his kingdom to all nations. The mission, once confined to Israel, now explodes across the globe. The book ends where it began, with the promise of "Still Emmanuel"—God with us. Jesus promises his abiding presence to empower the Church's mission, reminding us that we are never alone in building his kingdom.

Conclusion: The Enduring Lessons from "God With Us"

Living in the Kingdom Today

Edward Sri's journey through Matthew's Gospel leaves us with profound lessons for our own lives. The author wants us to understand that Christianity is not a set of abstract beliefs, but an encounter with a person: Jesus Christ, the King who is "God with us." The primary lessons are:

Core Takeaways for the Modern Disciple:

  • See with New Eyes: We must learn to read the Bible through the lens of the Old Testament and the Jewish context. Doing so unlocks a rich tapestry of meaning and reveals the stunning coherence of God's plan for salvation.
  • The Kingdom is Now: The Kingdom of God is not just a future hope; it is a present reality inaugurated by Jesus. We are called to live as its citizens, embodying its counter-cultural values of mercy, forgiveness, and sacrificial love in a world that prizes power and self-interest.
  • We Have a Role to Play: Just as Jesus commissioned his apostles, he commissions us. We are called to be "light to the world," extending the healing and forgiveness of the kingdom to our families, workplaces, and communities.
  • We Are Not Alone: The promise of "Emmanuel" is for us today. Christ is with us in the Church, in the Scriptures, in the poor, and most profoundly in the Eucharist. This divine presence is the source of our strength and the guarantee of our hope.

Ultimately, "God With Us" is a call to move beyond a superficial faith. It challenges us to know Jesus not just as a historical figure, but as our personal King, and to understand that every part of our lives is an opportunity to participate in his ongoing mission to bring all of creation back to the Father.
