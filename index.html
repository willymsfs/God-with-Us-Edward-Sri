God With Us

Encountering Jesus in the Gospel of Matthew

An exploration based on the work of Edward Sri

Introduction: The King and His Kingdom

The Gospel of Matthew presents Jesus as Emmanuel—"God with us." This is not just a title, but the key to his entire mission. He is the long-awaited Messiah-King, fulfilling centuries of Old Testament prophecy to establish a kingdom that restores humanity's relationship with God. This journey explores how every word, action, and detail of Jesus's life fits into this grand, divine plan.

Chapter 1: The Return of the King

The Power of a Genealogy

Matthew's Gospel begins not with a story, but with a genealogy. For its original audience, this was a headline announcement: God's plan is reaching its climax. It establishes Jesus as the "Son of David," the rightful heir to Israel's throne. The structure itself, built around the number fourteen (the numerical value of David's name in Hebrew ), powerfully proclaims Jesus as the ultimate Davidic king who has come to end the long exile of his people.

Modern Comparison: A Royal Proclamation

Imagine a nation that has been without its ruling family for centuries, living under foreign occupation. Suddenly, a document is published proving the existence of a true heir. That is the power of Matthew 1. It's not a dry family tree; it's a declaration of hope and the return of the true king.

Chapter 3: The King's Anointing

Baptism as a Royal Coronation

Jesus's baptism is his public anointing as Messiah-King. John the Baptist, acting as the new Elijah, prepares the way for a "New Exodus"—a spiritual liberation. When the Holy Spirit descends upon Jesus, it mirrors the anointing of Israel's ancient kings. The Father's voice from heaven confirms his identity as the beloved Son and Servant-Messiah prophesied by Isaiah. This is not an act of repentance for sin, but the inauguration of his royal mission.

The Spirit descends not just as a dove, but as an anointing oil, consecrating the King for his mission.

Chapter 5: The Challenge of the Kingdom

The Sermon on the Mount's Radical Vision

The Sermon on the Mount is a counter-cultural manifesto. To an audience expecting a military messiah to overthrow Rome, Jesus offers a shocking alternative: "Blessed are the peacemakers," "Love your enemy." He challenges the prevailing responses to oppression—violent revolution, rigid separation, or total withdrawal—and calls Israel back to its true mission: to be a light to all nations. He presents a kingdom built not on power, but on mercy, forgiveness, and sacrificial love.

Modern Example: Non-Violent Revolution

Think of leaders like Martin Luther King Jr. or Mahatma Gandhi, who confronted oppressive systems not with weapons, but with a radical commitment to love and non-violence. Jesus's sermon was a call for this kind of spiritual revolution, one that transforms hearts before it transforms society.

Chapter 9: The Keys to the Kingdom

Peter, the Royal Prime Minister

When Jesus gives Peter the "keys of the kingdom," he is using powerful Old Testament imagery. In the Davidic Kingdom (Isaiah 22 ), the "keys" were the symbol of the prime minister—the highest official who governed with the king's own authority. Jesus, the new Davidic King, is establishing his new kingdom, the Church, and appointing Peter as his prime minister. The name "Peter" (Rock) signifies that he will be the firm foundation for this Church, holding back the forces of death just as the Temple's foundation stone was believed to do.

The Keys represent divine authority; the Rock represents an unshakeable foundation.

Chapter 13: The Climax of the Cross

Defeat as Victory, the Cross as a Throne

The Cross is not Jesus's defeat, but his enthronement as King. Here, he becomes the New Passover Lamb, whose sacrifice establishes a New Covenant and frees humanity from sin. He enters into solidarity with his people's suffering, taking on the curses of the covenant with Adam and Israel to reverse them. The Crucifixion is the ultimate fulfillment of the sacrifice of Isaac, where God the Father offers His beloved Son to bring about the promised blessing for all nations. It is the moment Israel's light shines brightest, piercing the darkness of the world.

Chapter 14: The Great Commission

A Kingdom for All Nations

The Gospel culminates with the resurrected Jesus, now vested with all authority, commissioning his apostles to extend his kingdom to the ends of the earth. The mission, once focused on the "lost sheep of Israel," now bursts forth to "all nations." The book ends as it began, with the promise of Emmanuel: "I am with you always, to the close of the age." This promise of Christ's presence—in the Church, in the sacraments, and in his people—is the power that fuels the mission.
